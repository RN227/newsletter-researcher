# ☕ AI in the news

**Ads Are Coming to Your ChatGPT Conversations**

OpenAI has started showing ads to free and low-cost ChatGPT users in the US — meaning the AI assistant you've been chatting with for free is now, officially, ad-supported. It's a familiar trade-off: you get the tool for free, and in return, advertisers get access to your attention.

This matters because ChatGPT has hundreds of millions of users, so this isn't a small experiment — it's a major shift in how one of the world's most-used AI tools makes money. It also raises questions about whether ads could eventually shape or influence the answers ChatGPT gives you.

*If you use ChatGPT's free tier, expect your experience to start looking a lot more like Google Search — ads included — and it may be worth keeping an eye on whether that changes the quality or neutrality of responses.*

**The US Military Is Going All-In on One AI Company**

The Pentagon has officially adopted Palantir's AI software as a core system for the US military — meaning one private company's technology will now sit at the heart of America's defence operations. Palantir specialises in crunching massive amounts of data to help organisations make decisions, and the military has been one of its biggest customers for years.

This is a big deal because it signals just how deeply AI is being woven into national security — not as a future possibility, but as present-day infrastructure. It also raises serious questions about accountability: when AI helps make military decisions, who's responsible if something goes wrong?

*AI moving into the military mainstream isn't just a tech story — it's a sign that governments are treating AI as essential infrastructure, which will likely accelerate regulation, debate, and public scrutiny around how these systems are built and governed.*

**Google Is Making It Easy to Pack Up and Move to Its AI Chatbot**

Google has launched tools that let you import your chat history and personal information from other AI chatbots — like ChatGPT — directly into Gemini, its own AI assistant. Think of it like being able to take your contacts and messages with you when you switch phone carriers.

This matters because one of the biggest reasons people stick with any app is the hassle of starting over somewhere new. By removing that friction, Google is making a serious play to win over users who've already built habits with rival chatbots — and it could shake up which AI assistant ends up dominating your daily life.

*The AI chatbot wars are heating up, and the battlefield is now your loyalty — expect more tools designed to make switching easier, which is ultimately good news for users who want more choice and less lock-in.*
