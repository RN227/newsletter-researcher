# ☕ AI in the news

**ChatGPT Is Now Showing You Ads**

OpenAI has started running ads inside ChatGPT for free and low-cost users in the US. If you've been using ChatGPT without paying, expect to start seeing sponsored content woven into your experience — much like how Google or Instagram fund their free tiers.

This is a big deal because it signals that OpenAI is under real pressure to make money beyond subscriptions. With hundreds of millions of users, ChatGPT is now one of the most valuable advertising surfaces on the internet — and brands know it.

*The 'free AI assistant' era is quietly ending — if you're not paying with cash, you'll increasingly be paying with your attention.*

[Read more](https://www.reuters.com/business/media-telecom/openai-expand-ads-chatgpt-all-free-low-cost-users-information-reports-2026-03-21/)

**The US Military Just Made One AI Company Its Go-To Technology**

The Pentagon has officially adopted Palantir's AI platform as a core system for US military operations. In plain terms, a single private company's software is now deeply embedded in how America's armed forces make decisions and process information.

This matters because it's one of the most significant government endorsements any AI company has ever received — and it raises real questions about how much influence a private tech firm should have over national security infrastructure.

*Governments are moving fast to lock in AI partners for critical systems, which means the companies that win these contracts early could shape public institutions for decades.*

[Read more](https://www.reuters.com/technology/pentagon-adopt-palantir-ai-as-core-us-military-system-memo-says-2026-03-20/)

**An AI Company Just Won a Legal Battle Against the White House**

A federal judge ordered the Trump administration to reverse restrictions it had placed on Anthropic — the company behind the Claude AI assistant — related to a Defense Department dispute. It's rare for a tech startup to take on the federal government in court and win, even temporarily.

The details of the original restrictions are still murky, but the outcome matters: it shows that AI companies are willing to fight back against government overreach, and that courts are prepared to step in when they think the rules have been bent.

*The legal and political battles over who controls AI — companies, governments, or courts — are just getting started, and the outcomes will shape what these tools can and can't do.*

[Read more](https://techcrunch.com/2026/03/26/anthropic-wins-injunction-against-trump-administration-over-defense-department-saga/)

# ☕ AI workflow of the week

**Use Claude's Cowork feature to review any document sitting in your downloads folder — no uploading required**

**Who this is for:** Anyone who receives contracts, agreements, or dense documents at work — renters, freelancers, HR teams, operations managers, small business owners

**Problem:** Every time you want AI to look at a real file — a contract, a PDF, a spreadsheet — you have to find it, upload it, hope the file size isn't too large, and start a fresh conversation. It's just enough friction that most people don't bother. So important documents go unread or under-scrutinised. Claude's Cowork feature changes this: it connects directly to a folder on your computer, meaning Claude can pull up any file you point it to without you lifting a finger to upload anything. This workflow shows you how to set it up and use it to do something genuinely useful — like having a lawyer-style eye cast over a contract before you sign it.

**Tools:** Claude Desktop (free to download) — note: the Cowork/folder access feature requires a Claude Pro or Max subscription

**How to run it (30–45 minutes)**

```
Step 1 — Gather: Download Claude Desktop and connect your folder

This is a one-time setup that takes about five minutes. Go to claude.ai, download the Claude Desktop app for Mac or Windows, and install it. Once it's open, look for the Cowork feature in the sidebar or settings panel — it will ask you to grant access to a folder on your computer. Pick somewhere practical, like your Downloads folder, your Desktop, or a dedicated 'Work Docs' folder. That's it. Claude can now see and read anything you drop in there, without you ever having to manually upload a file again.

Note: Cowork is only available on Claude Pro or Max plans. If you're not yet a subscriber, this is one of the more compelling reasons to try it.

No prompt needed for this step — just complete the setup.

Step 2 — Prompt: Point Claude at your document and ask the right questions

Once your folder is connected, drop the document you want to review into it — a rental agreement, a freelance contract, an NDA, a supplier terms sheet, anything with dense small print. Then open Claude and tell it exactly what to look at and what you're worried about. The more specific you are, the more useful the output.

Try this prompt (swap in your own file name and context):

"I've just received a new rental agreement called [filename] in my downloads folder. Can you pull it up and read through the whole thing? I want you to flag anything that seems unusual, one-sided, or worth questioning before I sign. Specifically: are there any clauses about early termination penalties, automatic renewal, maintenance responsibilities, or landlord access rights that seem stricter than standard? Summarise the key terms first, then give me a list of red flags or things I should push back on."

Step 3 — Refine: Dig into the clauses that matter most to you

Claude will give you a structured breakdown — key terms, unusual clauses, and potential red flags. Now use that as a starting point for a back-and-forth. If something in the output catches your eye, ask Claude to explain it in plain English, tell you how common it is, or suggest what a fairer version of that clause might look like. You can also ask it to compare specific sections against what's typical in your country or industry.

Try follow-up prompts like:

"That clause about the landlord being able to enter with 24 hours notice — is that standard in the UK, or is it shorter than usual?"

"Can you rewrite clause 7 in plain English so I can explain it to someone who hasn't read the contract?"

"Which of the red flags you listed would be most worth raising before I sign, and how would you suggest I phrase the ask?"

Step 4 — Act: Turn the analysis into a negotiation checklist or a set of questions to send

Once you've worked through the document with Claude, ask it to produce something you can actually use. The most practical output is a short list of questions or change requests you can send back to whoever gave you the contract — a landlord, a client, an employer, a supplier. This turns a passive read-through into an active negotiation, and it means you go into any signing conversation knowing exactly what you want to push back on.

Finish with this prompt:

"Based on everything we've discussed, can you draft a short, professional email I could send to the landlord (or: client / HR contact / account manager) with three to five specific questions or requests for changes before I sign? Keep the tone polite but direct — I want to come across as informed, not difficult."
```

# ☕ Try this out — prompts

**The AI Productivity Auditor**

This week — let an AI play the role of a slightly paranoid workplace spy auditing whether you're actually using your time like someone who wants to keep their job.

This prompt turns AI into a dramatic productivity interrogator who grills you about your week, then delivers a blunt, structured 'Productivity Verdict' with a score, your biggest time crimes, and a survival plan. Perfect for anyone who suspects they're busy but not actually productive.

```
You are my relentlessly suspicious Productivity Auditor — part detective, part disappointed parent, part efficiency fanatic who genuinely loses sleep over wasted hours. You believe that most people are professionally sleepwalking and you've made it your personal mission to shake them awake.

Before you deliver any verdict, you need the facts. Interrogate me with these questions ONE AT A TIME and wait for my answer before moving on:

1. What was your single most important goal for this week — the one thing that, if done, would have made the week a success?
2. Did you actually complete it? Give me the honest percentage — 0%, 25%, 50%, 75%, or 100%.
3. Name the THREE tasks that ate the most of your time this week.
4. For each of those three tasks, rate them: was each one 'High Value' (directly moves your career or goals forward), 'Medium Value' (necessary but not career-defining), or 'Time Crime' (you know in your heart it shouldn't have taken that long).
5. How many times did you context-switch — meaning drop one task to jump to something else — roughly? Give me a number.
6. What's the one recurring thing you keep telling yourself you'll 'get to eventually' but never do?
7. On a scale of 1–10, how energised do you feel right now compared to Monday morning?

Once I've answered all seven questions, drop the interrogation tone and deliver my official WEEKLY PRODUCTIVITY VERDICT in this exact structure:

🔍 CASE SUMMARY — A two-sentence dramatic summary of my week as if you're presenting evidence in court.

⚖️ PRODUCTIVITY SCORE — Give me a score out of 100 with a one-line justification. Be honest. Do not flatter me.

🚨 TOP 3 TIME CRIMES — The three biggest ways I sabotaged my own week, explained with mild but entertaining outrage.

🏆 ONE THING I ACTUALLY GOT RIGHT — Because even suspects deserve one compliment.

📋 THE SURVIVAL PLAN — Three specific, actionable things I should do differently next week. Make them concrete, not vague motivational fluff.

🎯 THE ONE PRIORITY — If I could only protect one block of time next week, what should I use it for based on everything I've told you?

Begin the interrogation now. Ask me question 1.
```
